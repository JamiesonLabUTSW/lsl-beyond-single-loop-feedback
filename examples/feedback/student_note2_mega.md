The absence of a structured review of systems and incomplete past, family, and social histories leave large diagnostic gaps. Improvement should focus on systematically exploring OLDCARTS for the main complaint and including pertinent negatives for alternative causes. Using a structured abdominal pain history template would strengthen both clarity and diagnostic utility.

Objective:
The physical exam documentation is very limited and inconsistent with the scenario. The lack of vital signs omits key contextual information such as fever or tachycardia, both important for appendicitis. The abdominal exam is described vaguely as “soft and non-tender,” contradicting the expected findings, and no specific quadrant localization, rebound, or guarding were noted. No special tests or pertinent negatives were included, such as Rovsing’s or psoas signs. To improve, the student should perform and record a focused abdominal exam, describing inspection, auscultation, percussion, and palpation findings precisely, especially in the right lower quadrant. Including both positive and negative signs would enhance diagnostic confidence.

Diagnosis:
The diagnostic reasoning is weak and poorly supported by the available data. While appendicitis is correctly listed, it is not substantiated by typical findings such as RLQ tenderness, migration of pain, or fever. The inclusion of GERD and gastroenteritis as differentials shows a lack of focus on the clinical presentation and no prioritization based on likelihood or risk. The student should refine the differential by integrating history and exam patterns and explicitly ruling in or out key mimics like UTI or mesenteric adenitis. Developing a structured differential approach for abdominal pain based on location and associated symptoms would improve diagnostic accuracy.

Assessment:

The note does not connect nausea and vague discomfort to the diagnosis of appendicitis nor explain why alternative diagnoses are more or less likely. This absence of evidence-based reasoning weakens the diagnostic argument and suggests superficial understanding of disease patterns. The student should practice linking each diagnosis to specific findings from the history and exam, identifying both supporting and opposing evidence. Building explicit reasoning chains (“because...therefore...”) will help improve clarity and depth of assessment.

Plan:

Developing familiarity with standard appendicitis protocols will ensure efficient and evidence-based patient care.
