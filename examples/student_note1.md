### **History:**

22-year-old male presents with 24-hour history of abdominal pain. Pain began as a dull ache near the umbilicus and migrated to the right lower quadrant, becoming sharp and constant. Associated nausea, one episode of vomiting, decreased appetite, and low-grade fever. No diarrhea, constipation, dysuria, or urinary frequency. No prior episodes of similar pain. No past surgeries, medications, or allergies. Denies alcohol, smoking, or drug use. No family history of GI disease.

---

### **Physical Exam:**

General: Appears uncomfortable, lying still in bed.
Vitals: T 38.2°C, HR 98 bpm, BP 118/74 mmHg, RR 18.
Abdomen: Not distended, minimal movement with respiration. Normal bowel sounds. Tenderness localized to right lower quadrant with guarding and rebound tenderness at McBurney’s point. Positive Rovsing’s sign. No hepatosplenomegaly or CVA tenderness.
Skin: No jaundice or rash.
Other systems: Normal.

---

### **Diagnosis #1: Acute Appendicitis**

#### **Supporting Findings:**

* Migration of pain from periumbilical area to right lower quadrant
* Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
* Low-grade fever
* RLQ tenderness with guarding and rebound
* Positive Rovsing’s sign
* Absence of urinary or bowel symptoms

---

### **Diagnosis #2: Gastroenteritis**

#### **Supporting Findings:**

* Abdominal pain and nausea could be consistent with viral gastroenteritis
* Mild fever

---

### **Diagnosis #3: Mesenteric Adenitis**

#### **Supporting Findings:**

* Could present with RLQ tenderness in young patients

---

### **Studies:**

* CBC with differential (expect leukocytosis)
* CRP and ESR
* Serum electrolytes and renal function
* Urinalysis to rule out UTI
* Pregnancy test if applicable
* Abdominal ultrasound (first-line imaging)
* CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast if diagnosis unclear
